Research Hypotheses

The empirical analysis is divided into two categories. The first set — Price Hypotheses (H0–H3) — tests whether data center intensity is associated with higher electricity costs for local residents and businesses. The second set — ICT Sector Hypotheses (H4–H6) — explores whether hosting data centers corresponds with broader shifts in the national ICT economy.

All hypotheses are tested using Spearman's rank correlation, chosen for its robustness to outliers and non-normal distributions. For statistically significant results, OLS regression with GDP per capita as a control variable is also applied.

Price Hypotheses — H0 to H3

Modern AI workloads require up to 100× more computational power than traditional operations, causing hyperscale data centers to dramatically shift regional electricity demand curves. These hypotheses test whether this demand pressure is visible in electricity prices paid by households and businesses.

H0: Household electricity prices × DC energy consumption share

H1 (alternative): There is a positive correlation between household electricity prices and the data center share of national energy consumptcoeion.

H0 (null): There is no correlation.

Expected direction Positive ↑
Rationale

A higher share of national electricity consumed by data centers indicates systemic demand pressure on the grid. This hypothesis examines whether that pressure translates into higher costs for everyday households.

H1: Household electricity prices × data centers per capita

H1 (alternative): There is a positive correlation between household electricity prices and the number of data centers per million inhabitants.

H0 (null): There is no correlation.

Expected direction Positive ↑
Rationale

Regions with a greater number of data centers relative to their population may experience localized infrastructure strain. This hypothesis captures potential regional effects not visible at the aggregate energy level.

H2: Non-household electricity prices × DC energy consumption share

H1 (alternative): There is a positive correlation between non-household electricity prices and the data center share of national energy consumption.

H0 (null): There is no correlation.

Expected direction Positive ↑
Rationale

Commercial and industrial consumers operate under different pricing mechanisms than households, often being more exposed to market dynamics. This hypothesis evaluates whether data center energy demand is reflected in business electricity tariffs.

H3: Non-household electricity prices × data centers per capita

H1 (alternative): There is a positive correlation between non-household electricity prices and the number of data centers per million inhabitants.

H0 (null): There is no correlation.

Expected direction Positive ↑
Rationale

The geographic concentration of data centers can create localized demand shocks. This hypothesis tests whether clustering is linked to electricity prices for non-household consumers and whether the burden falls differently across consumer groups.

ICT Sector Hypotheses — H4 to H6

While data centers require minimal permanent local workforce, this set of hypotheses tests whether extreme infrastructural density functions as a broader economic anchor — driving ICT employment, gross value added, and R&D investment at the national level. This directly addresses the "job paradox" discussed in the literature.

H4: ICT Gross Value Added × data center concentration

H1 (alternative): There is a positive correlation between a country's ICT sector GVA and its data center concentration (tested against both DC_SHARE and DC_CAPITA).

H0 (null): There is no correlation.

Expected direction Positive ↑

Extensive data center infrastructure may contribute to the overall economic output of the ICT sector. This hypothesis determines whether data centers act as drivers of measurable economic productivity at the national level.

H5: ICT employment share × data center concentration

H1 (alternative): There is a positive correlation between the share of ICT employment and data center concentration.

H0 (null): There is no correlation.

Expected direction Positive ↑

Despite their scale, data centers require relatively few direct workers once operational. This hypothesis examines whether their presence is nevertheless associated with broader ICT job creation through indirect or spillover effects.

H6: ICT R&D spending × data center concentration

H1 (alternative): There is a positive correlation between ICT R&D spending (BERD) and data center concentration.

H0 (null): There is no correlation.

Expected direction Positive ↑

Sustaining and expanding data center infrastructure requires continuous technological innovation. This hypothesis explores whether higher data center concentration is linked to increased ICT R&D spending — testing whether capital intensity translates into stronger innovation activity.
