Price Hypotheses · H0 – H3

Spearman's Correlation — Energy Prices

H0 and H1 are statistically significant at the p<0.001 level, indicating a moderate positive relationship between household electricity prices and data center concentration. H2 and H3 (non-household prices) are not statistically significant.

Hypothesis x (independent) y (dependent) ρ p-value n Significance
H0 HH_E_P DC_CAPITA 0.550 0.000613 31 *** p<0.001
H1 HH_E_P DC_SHARE 0.573 0.000397 29 *** p<0.001
H2 NON-HH_E_P DC_CAPITA 0.298 0.091579 29 † p<0.10
H3 NON-HH_E_P DC_SHARE 0.299 0.091488 29 † p<0.10

OLS Regression — H0 & H1 (statistically significant results only)

For H0 and H1, OLS regression was run with and without GDP per capita as a control variable. Including GDP per capita substantially improved explanatory power, suggesting that economic development — rather than data centers directly — partially explains higher electricity prices.

Model Hypothesis Variables R² Adjusted R² n (after IQR)
Simple OLS H0 HH_E_P ~ DC_SHARE 0.145 — 31
OLS + GDP control H0 HH_E_P ~ DC_SHARE + GDP_PC 0.292 — 31
Simple OLS H1 HH_E_P ~ DC_CAPITA 0.161 — 29
OLS + GDP control H1 HH_E_P ~ DC_CAPITA + GDP_PC 0.319 — 29

Interpretation

H0 and H1 cannot be rejected — there is a statistically significant positive relationship between household electricity prices and data center concentration. However, when GDP per capita is controlled for, explanatory power increases substantially (R² rises from ~0.15 to ~0.31), indicating that more economically developed countries tend to have both more data centers and higher energy prices. Data centers are therefore associated with higher prices, but are not necessarily the direct cause. H2 and H3 yield no statistically significant findings — no clear relationship between non-household prices and data center concentration is observable.

ICT Hypotheses · H4 – H6

Spearman's Correlation — ICT Sector

Results for ICT hypotheses show high variability. H5 (ICT employment × DC per capita) is the strongest finding: a highly significant positive correlation (ρ = 0.703, p < 0.001). H6b (ICT R&D × DC per capita) also shows a significant positive correlation. H4 (ICT GVA) yields weak or insignificant results.

Interpretation

H5 yields the strongest finding of the entire analysis: countries with more data centers per capita also have a significantly higher ICT employment share (ρ = 0.703, p < 0.001). This suggests that data center infrastructure does act as a broader economic anchor for the ICT sector, even if individual facilities require few direct employees.

H6b similarly shows a significant positive correlation between DC per capita and ICT R&D spending, suggesting that dense infrastructure drives capital into innovation. H4 (GVA) shows only weak, borderline results, indicating that energy consumption alone does not guarantee proportionate growth in economic output.

Outlier Analysis

Key Outlier Countries

The IQR method identified several countries as statistical outliers due to extreme data center concentration relative to their population. These countries were excluded from OLS regression but retained in Spearman correlation analysis.

🇮🇪 Ireland

Data centers consume an estimated 18–22% of national electricity — the highest share in Europe. 95 out of 127 data centers are concentrated in Dublin. Removed from OLS as DC_SHARE outlier.

🇳🇱 Netherlands

Data centers account for approximately 5.2% of national energy demand, driven by the Amsterdam hub. Identified as outlier in DC_SHARE analysis.

🇩🇰 Denmark

Data centers account for approximately 4.5% of national demand, above the European median. Flagged by IQR in energy share analysis.

🇱🇺 Luxembourg & 🇱🇮 Liechtenstein

Very small populations produce extreme DC per capita values that heavily distort the distribution. Flagged and removed in DC_CAPITA regression models.
